Introduction: Why Structure Matters

Most people react to situations. They see a problem and immediately start generating solutions. They feel the pressure of a deadline and start working faster. They notice a competitor's move and scramble to respond.

This is natural. It's also almost always wrong.

The best strategic thinkers do something different. Before they act, before they even consider options, they pause to see the structure of the situation. They ask: What forces are at play here? What constraints exist? Where are the leverage points?

This module teaches you to see structure. It's the foundation of everything else in strategic thinking.

Part 1: The Diagnosis Framework

Richard Rumelt, in his book Good Strategy Bad Strategy, identifies the core of strategy as having three parts: a diagnosis, a guiding policy, and coherent actions. Most people skip straight to actions. The best strategists spend most of their time on diagnosis.

A diagnosis is not a description of the problem. It's an interpretation of the situation that identifies the critical factors. It answers the question: "What's really going on here?"

Key Insight: A good diagnosis simplifies complexity by identifying the one or two factors that matter most. It's an act of judgment, not just analysis.

The Three Questions of Diagnosis

When facing any strategic situation, ask:

  1. What is the challenge? Not the symptom, but the underlying obstacle that makes this situation difficult.
  2. Why does this challenge exist? What structural factors created and sustain it?
  3. What would have to change? If you could change one thing about the structure, what would unlock progress?

These questions seem simple. They're not. Most people answer the first question with a symptom ("sales are down") rather than a challenge ("we're competing on price in a commodity market with higher-cost operations than our competitors").

Part 2: Structural Analysis

Structure is the arrangement of parts that determines how a system behaves. In business, structure includes things like: market dynamics, competitive positions, organizational capabilities, regulatory constraints, and technological possibilities.

The key insight of structural thinking is that behavior follows structure. If you want to change outcomes, you usually need to change structure, not just work harder within the existing structure.

Symptoms vs. Root Causes

A symptom is something you can observe directly. A root cause is the structural factor that produces the symptom. The relationship is often non-obvious.

Example:

Symptom: High employee turnover

Obvious interpretation: We need better retention programs

Structural analysis: Our compensation is tied to individual performance in a role that requires collaboration. The structure punishes the behaviors we need.

Root cause: Misaligned incentive structure

Notice that the structural analysis completely changes what you would do. Retention programs treat the symptom. Restructuring incentives addresses the cause.

The Five Structural Forces

When analyzing any situation, look for these five types of structural forces:

  1. Incentives: What behaviors does the current structure reward and punish?
  2. Constraints: What limits exist on possible actions? (Resources, regulations, physics, time)
  3. Feedback loops: What reinforcing or balancing dynamics are at play?
  4. Dependencies: What must happen before other things can happen?
  5. Information flows: Who knows what, and when do they know it?

Part 3: Leverage Points

Donella Meadows, the systems thinker, defined leverage points as "places within a complex system where a small shift in one thing can produce big changes in everything."

Not all interventions are equal. Some actions produce disproportionate results because they change the structure of the system, not just its current state.

The Leverage Hierarchy

From weakest to strongest, here are types of interventions:

  1. Parameters: Changing numbers (budgets, targets, prices). Easy but rarely transformative.
  2. Buffers: Changing the size of stabilizing stocks. Moderately effective.
  3. Structure: Changing the physical or informational flows. Powerful but difficult.
  4. Rules: Changing the incentives and constraints. Very powerful.
  5. Goals: Changing what the system is trying to achieve. Transformative.
  6. Paradigms: Changing the mindset out of which the system arises. Revolutionary.
Key Insight: Most people focus on parameters (the weakest leverage) because they're easiest to change. Strategic thinkers focus on rules, goals, and paradigms because that's where lasting change happens.

Part 4: The Clarity Test

How do you know when your structural analysis is good enough? Apply the Clarity Test:

The Four Criteria

  1. Specificity: Could someone else identify the same structural factors from your description? Vague analyses like "we need better communication" fail this test.
  2. Mechanism: Can you explain how the structure produces the observed outcomes? If you can't trace the causal chain, you don't understand the structure.
  3. Prediction: Does your analysis predict what would happen if the structure changed? Good structural understanding enables prediction.
  4. Non-obviousness: Would someone without your analysis reach different conclusions? If your diagnosis is obvious, you probably haven't gone deep enough.

Most analyses fail at least one of these criteria. That's fine—it tells you where to dig deeper. Keep refining until your analysis passes all four.

Putting It Together

Structural clarity is not a one-time analysis. It's a way of seeing. With practice, you'll start to automatically notice structural factors in every situation you encounter.

The goal is not to have a perfect analysis before acting. It's to have a clear enough understanding of the structure that you can identify high-leverage interventions and predict their likely effects.

This takes practice. The assignment below is designed to help you develop this skill.

Your Assignment

Choose a real situation you're currently facing—professional or personal. It should be something genuinely challenging, where you haven't yet found a satisfying solution.

Write a structural analysis (500-800 words) that includes:

  1. A clear diagnosis: What is the core challenge (not symptom)?
  2. Structural factors: What 2-3 structural forces are most important?
  3. A leverage point: Where could a small change produce disproportionate results?

Your analysis should pass the Clarity Test:

  • Specific enough that someone else could identify the same factors
  • Explains the mechanism by which structure produces outcomes
  • Makes a prediction about what would change if the structure changed
  • Offers insight that isn't immediately obvious
